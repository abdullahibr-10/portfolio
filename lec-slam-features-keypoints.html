Features — Keypoints

Nov 2025 MSc Mobile Robotics, UniBonn

Feature-based visual SLAM and SfM require detecting stable, distinctive image locations — keypoints — that can be reliably found across frames with different viewpoints, scales, and illumination. This lecture covers the main keypoint detectors and what makes a good keypoint.

1. Properties of a Good Keypoint

  • Repeatability: the same physical point should be detected across different views of the scene, even with viewpoint change, scale change, and illumination variation.
  • Distinctiveness: the local appearance around the keypoint should be unique enough to distinguish it from other keypoints in the image.
  • Locality: the keypoint should have compact support — its description should depend on a small image region.
  • Quantity: enough keypoints must be detected to have redundant coverage of the scene.
  • Efficiency: detection should be fast enough for real-time SLAM (30+ frames per second).

2. Harris Corner Detector

Harris & Stephens (1988) detect corners by analysing the local gradient structure. The structure tensor (second-moment matrix) at each pixel is:

M = Σ_window [ Ix²   IxIy ; IxIy   Iy² ]

where Ix, Iy are image gradients. The eigenvalues λ₁, λ₂ of M describe the gradient energy in the two principal directions. A corner has both λ₁ and λ₂ large; an edge has one large and one small; a flat region has both small. The Harris response function avoids explicit eigendecomposition:

R = det(M) − k · trace(M)² = λ₁λ₂ − k(λ₁+λ₂)²

with k ≈ 0.04–0.06. Non-maximum suppression retains local maxima of R above a threshold as keypoints. Harris is rotation-invariant but not scale-invariant.

3. Scale Space and LoG

To detect scale-invariant features, build a scale space by convolving the image with Gaussians at multiple scales σ: L(x, y, σ) = G(x, y, σ) * I(x, y). The Laplacian of Gaussian (LoG) ∇²L detects blob-like structures. A feature is a local maximum in the 3D space (x, y, σ) — this determines both the location and the characteristic scale σ* at which the blob is most prominent.

4. SIFT Keypoints — DoG Detector

SIFT (Lowe, 2004) approximates LoG with the Difference of Gaussians (DoG): D(x, y, σ) = L(x, y, kσ) − L(x, y, σ). This is a band-pass filter and is much faster than LoG. Keypoints are detected as local extrema in the (x, y, σ) DoG pyramid. Two additional filtering steps are applied:

  • Low-contrast rejection: remove candidates with |D| < threshold.
  • Edge rejection: compute the Hessian of D at the candidate location. If the ratio of principal curvatures (largest/smallest eigenvalue) exceeds a threshold r_th, the point is on an edge, not a corner, and is discarded.

5. FAST Keypoints

FAST (Rosten & Drummond, 2006) trades accuracy for extreme speed — critical for real-time SLAM on embedded hardware. A pixel p is a corner if there exist at least N = 9 consecutive pixels on a circle of radius 3 around p that are all brighter than I(p) + t or all darker than I(p) − t (threshold t ≈ 10). A decision tree over the 16 circle pixels accelerates the test. FAST detects thousands of corners in milliseconds but has no natural scale information — scale must be handled via an image pyramid.

Scale Selection The characteristic scale of a SIFT keypoint is the DoG pyramid scale σ* that maximises |D(x*, y*, σ)|. This σ* is used to normalise the patch size when computing the descriptor: the descriptor window is always 16σ* × 16σ* pixels, making the descriptor scale-invariant. FAST keypoints use the pyramid level index as a proxy for scale.

In practice, ORB (which combines FAST detection with BRIEF descriptors) is the go-to choice for real-time SLAM (ORB-SLAM2/3), while SIFT remains the gold standard for offline SfM (COLMAP). The next lecture covers how to describe the local patch around each detected keypoint.
