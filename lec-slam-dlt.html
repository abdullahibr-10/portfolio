DLT — Direct Linear Transform

Oct 2025 MSc Mobile Robotics, UniBonn

The Direct Linear Transform is a foundational algorithm in computer vision that computes the homography between two point sets from point correspondences. It forms the backbone of camera calibration (Zhang's method), image mosaicing, AR marker tracking, and forms the basis for estimating the fundamental matrix.

1. Homographies

A homography H is a 3×3 matrix (defined up to scale, 8 DOF) that maps points between two projective planes. In homogeneous coordinates: p' = Hp. Homographies arise naturally when: (1) the scene is planar, (2) the camera undergoes a pure rotation, or (3) we relate two images of the same plane (e.g., a flat floor, a calibration target, a wall). Given at least 4 point correspondences (x, x') with no three collinear, H can be recovered uniquely.

2. The DLT Formulation

Each correspondence (xᵢ, x'ᵢ) gives the constraint x'ᵢ × (H xᵢ) = 0 (cross product is zero since they are equal in projective space). Expanding this cross product yields 2 independent linear equations in the 9 entries of H. For n ≥ 4 point pairs, we stack these into a 2n×9 system Ah = 0, where h = vec(H) is the 9-vector of H entries arranged column by column.

3. SVD Solution

The homogeneous system Ah = 0 is solved via Singular Value Decomposition. Compute A = UΣVᵀ. The solution h is the last column of V (the right singular vector corresponding to the smallest singular value of A). This minimises ||Ah||² subject to ||h|| = 1 — the algebraic error. Reshape h into a 3×3 matrix to recover H.

When noise is present, the system is overdetermined (2n > 9 equations for n > 4 points) and has no exact solution. SVD finds the least-squares solution in the algebraic sense.

4. Normalisation (Hartley Normalisation)

Raw pixel coordinates span [0, image_width] × [0, image_height]. When these large numbers appear in matrix A, numerical conditioning is poor — small errors in image coordinates lead to large errors in H. Hartley's normalisation corrects this:

  1. Translate points so their centroid is at the origin.
  2. Scale uniformly so the mean distance from the origin is √2.
  3. Apply DLT to the normalised point sets to get H_norm.
  4. Denormalise: H = T'⁻¹ H_norm T, where T and T' are the normalisation transforms for the two point sets.

This simple preprocessing dramatically improves accuracy — a well-conditioned matrix A gives a stable SVD solution. Always normalise when implementing DLT in practice.

5. Recovering the Camera Matrix

If the intrinsic matrix K is known and we have correspondences between a planar world (Z=0) and the image, the homography H = K[r₁ r₂ t] where r₁, r₂ are the first two columns of the rotation matrix R. We recover:

  • [r₁ r₂ t] = K⁻¹ H
  • r₃ = r₁ × r₂ (completes the rotation matrix)
  • Scale: ||r₁|| = ||r₂|| = 1 (enforce this since K⁻¹H has an arbitrary scale)

This is exactly what Zhang's calibration does for each calibration board view, then refines jointly with non-linear optimisation.

6. Applications

  • Image mosaicing / panoramas: stitch images taken with a rotating camera using homographies estimated from matched features.
  • AR marker tracking: each frame, compute H between the known marker pattern and its image, recover pose.
  • Perspective correction: rectify images of documents or whiteboards photographed at an angle.
  • Planar SLAM: in environments with dominant planes (floors, walls), homographies provide cheap pose constraints without full 3D structure recovery.
DLT Limitations DLT minimises algebraic error, not geometric error (reprojection error). For precision applications, always refine the DLT result with a non-linear optimiser minimising symmetric transfer error or reprojection error. The DLT result is a good initialisation for this refinement.
