Bundle Adjustment

Oct 2025 MSc Mobile Robotics, UniBonn

Bundle Adjustment (BA) is the gold-standard back-end optimisation for Structure-from-Motion and SLAM. It jointly refines all camera poses and all 3D point positions to minimise the total reprojection error across all images and all visible points.

1. The BA Problem

Given N cameras with parameters {Pᵢ} and M 3D points {Xⱼ}, with visibility matrix W (wᵢⱼ = 1 if point j is observed in camera i), the objective is:

min Σᵢ Σⱼ wᵢⱼ · ρ( ||xᵢⱼ − π(Pᵢ, Xⱼ)||² )

where π(P, X) is the projection function, xᵢⱼ is the observed image point, and ρ is a robust loss function (identity for standard BA, Huber or Cauchy for robust BA). The variables being optimised are the camera parameters {Pᵢ} (typically 6 DOF pose + 4 DOF intrinsics = 10 per camera) and the point coordinates {Xⱼ} (3 DOF each).

2. Non-Linear Least Squares

BA is a non-linear least-squares (NLLS) problem. The Gauss-Newton method linearises the residuals via the Jacobian J = ∂r/∂θ and solves the normal equations at each iteration:

(JᵀJ) δ = −Jᵀ r

Levenberg-Marquardt (LM) adds a damping term λI to the normal equations: (JᵀJ + λI) δ = −Jᵀr. When λ is large, this is a small gradient descent step (safe but slow); when λ is small, it reduces to Gauss-Newton (fast but risky). LM adaptively adjusts λ based on whether the step decreased the cost, giving robust convergence from mediocre initialisations.

3. Jacobian Sparsity

The Jacobian J for BA is extremely sparse. A residual for point j in camera i depends only on camera i's parameters and point j's coordinates — not on any other camera or point. This means J has a specific 2-block structure with very few non-zeros. Naively inverting the dense JᵀJ matrix would cost O((10N + 3M)³) — infeasible for large reconstructions.

Partition the parameters into camera variables c (6 or 10 per camera) and point variables p (3 each). The Jacobian is J = [J_c | J_p].

4. The Schur Complement Trick

Partition the normal equations using the block structure:

[Jcᵀ Jc   Jcᵀ Jp] [δc] [Jcᵀ r]
[Jpᵀ Jc   Jpᵀ Jp] [δp] = [Jpᵀ r]

Since Jpᵀ Jp is block-diagonal (each point only interacts with itself), it is cheap to invert. Marginalising out δp (back-substitution) gives the reduced camera system (Schur complement):

S · δc = v,   where S = Jcᵀ Jc − Jcᵀ Jp (Jpᵀ Jp)⁻¹ Jpᵀ Jc

Solve the N×N camera-only system for δc, then back-substitute to get δp. This reduces the dominant cost from O((N+M)³) to O(N³ + NM), an enormous saving when M ≫ N (many more points than cameras).

5. Practical Considerations

  • Parameterisation: rotation must be parameterised carefully (Lie algebra se(3), angle-axis, or quaternions) to avoid singularities and maintain SO(3) constraints.
  • Sparse solvers: even S may be sparse (cameras only share constraints through observed points). Iterative solvers (PCG with Jacobi preconditioner) exploit this.
  • Initialisation quality: BA is local — a good initialisation from incremental SfM is essential to converge to the correct solution.
Convergence Check Monitor mean reprojection error per LM iteration. Good BA convergence typically reaches below 1 pixel mean error for well-calibrated cameras. Stagnation at high error (e.g., 5+ px) suggests outliers or poor initialisation — use robust loss functions or re-initialise.
