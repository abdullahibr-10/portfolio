Camera Parameters

Oct 2025 MSc Mobile Robotics, UniBonn

A camera transforms a 3D scene into a 2D image. To use cameras for SLAM, reconstruction, or manipulation, we need a precise mathematical model of this transformation. This lecture establishes the pinhole camera model and the calibration parameters that characterise a real lens.

1. The Pinhole Camera Model

The idealised pinhole camera models image formation as a perspective projection through a single point (the optical centre). A 3D point P = [X, Y, Z]ᵀ in camera coordinates projects to image coordinates [u, v]ᵀ according to:

u = f_x · (X/Z) + c_x,   v = f_y · (Y/Z) + c_y

Here f_x and f_y are the focal lengths in pixels (they differ if pixels are not square), and (c_x, c_y) is the principal point — ideally the image centre, but typically slightly off due to manufacturing tolerances.

2. The Intrinsic Matrix K

These parameters are compactly represented by the 3×3 intrinsic matrix K:

K = [[f_x, s, c_x], [0, f_y, c_y], [0, 0, 1]]

The skew parameter s accounts for non-rectangular pixels (usually s ≈ 0 in practice). In homogeneous coordinates, the projection becomes λp̃ = K · [I|0] · P̃, where λ is the projective depth and tildes denote homogeneous vectors. K has 5 degrees of freedom: f_x, f_y, c_x, c_y, s.

3. Extrinsic Parameters [R | t]

The camera rarely sits at the world origin. The extrinsic matrix [R | t] defines the rigid body transform from world coordinates to camera coordinates. R is a 3×3 rotation matrix (SO(3)) and t ∈ ℝ³ is the translation. The full projection equation is:

λ p̃ = K [R | t] P̃_world

Together, K, R, and t form the 3×4 camera projection matrix P = K[R|t]. Given P, we can predict the image location of any 3D world point.

4. Lens Distortion

Real lenses deviate from the ideal pinhole model. The two dominant distortion types are:

  • Radial distortion: causes straight lines to appear curved (barrel or pincushion). Modelled as: x_d = x(1 + k₁r² + k₂r⁴ + k₃r⁶) where r² = x² + y² and (x, y) are normalised image coordinates.
  • Tangential distortion: arises from imperfect lens-sensor alignment. Coefficients p₁, p₂ with: Δx = 2p₁xy + p₂(r²+2x²), Δy = p₁(r²+2y²) + 2p₂xy.

Distortion must be corrected before computing geometry (fundamental matrix, triangulation) as those algorithms assume pinhole projection. OpenCV's undistort() function handles this.

5. Camera Calibration — Zhang's Method

Zhang's method (1999) is the standard calibration procedure. A planar checkerboard pattern with known geometry is photographed from N ≥ 3 different orientations:

  1. Detect checkerboard corners (sub-pixel accuracy with iterative refinement).
  2. For each view, compute a homography H mapping the board plane to the image (the board is a planar scene — see next lecture).
  3. Each H provides two linear constraints on K. With N views, stack into a linear system and solve for K.
  4. Compute [R|t] for each view from K⁻¹H.
  5. Refine all parameters (K, distortion, all extrinsics) together with non-linear least squares (Levenberg-Marquardt) minimising reprojection error.
Focal Length: mm vs Pixels The physical focal length f_mm (millimetres) relates to the pixel focal length by f_x = f_mm / pixel_width_mm. For a sensor with 4µm pixels, f_mm = 4mm → f_x = 1000px. This conversion matters when comparing camera specs across different sensors.

Calibration accuracy directly affects all downstream geometry: a poorly calibrated camera gives poor triangulation, poor pose estimation, and poor depth accuracy. Always validate calibration by checking the mean reprojection error — below 0.5 pixels is good for most applications.
