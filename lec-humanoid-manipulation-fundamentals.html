Fundamentals of Manipulation

Nov 2025 MSc Mobile Robotics, UniBonn

Manipulation — grasping, moving, and controlling objects through physical contact — is one of the central challenges in robotics. This lecture establishes the mechanical and mathematical foundations: how grasps are classified, how contact forces are modelled, and what it means for a grasp to be stable and useful.

1. Grasp Taxonomy

Napier (1956) introduced the distinction between precision grasps and power grasps:

  • Precision grasp: object held between fingertips with minimal palm involvement. Allows fine motor control and repositioning. Examples: pinch grip (coin, pen tip), three-fingered tripod (pen writing).
  • Power grasp: object enveloped by whole hand including palm. Maximises grip force and stability. Examples: cylindrical grip (hammer handle), spherical grip (ball).

Cutkosky (1989) extended this into a 16-class taxonomy based on object geometry, size, and task requirements. This taxonomy is useful for matching object shape to appropriate hand configuration and is used in grasp dataset annotation.

2. Contact Models

How we model the force that a finger can exert at a contact point determines the grasp analysis approach:

  • Point Contact with Friction (PwF): simplest model. The contact can exert a force in any direction within the friction cone (half-angle arctan(μ), where μ is the Coulomb friction coefficient). No moment about the contact normal.
  • Soft Finger (SF): like PwF but also allows a moment about the contact normal (torsional friction). Models soft fingertip pads. Generates a wrench cone instead of a force cone.
  • Hard Finger (HF): frictionless point contact — can only push, not pull, and only along the contact normal. Requires many contact points for stability.

3. Form Closure

A grasp achieves form closure if the object is geometrically immobilised — no motion is possible regardless of the forces applied, purely due to the geometry of the contacts (no friction required). Form closure requires contact points that span all 6 DOF of possible object motion (3 translation, 3 rotation) from all sides. This typically requires 7+ contacts for a general 3D object. Form closure is very conservative and rarely the target in practice.

4. Force Closure

Force closure requires that the finger forces (lying within their respective friction cones) can resist any external wrench applied to the object. Formally, the grasp matrix G maps finger contact forces f to the resultant wrench w on the object: w = Gf. Force closure holds if the image of the friction cone polytope under G spans the 6D wrench space — i.e., any wrench can be balanced.

Force closure is achievable with as few as 2 contacts (antipodal grasp with sufficient friction). It is necessary for a practically useful grasp — but not sufficient, since kinematics and approach direction must also be feasible.

5. Friction Cone

For a contact point with coefficient of friction μ, the permissible reaction force f must satisfy:

||f_t|| ≤ μ · f_n

where f_n is the normal component and f_t is the tangential component. This defines a cone with half-angle arctan(μ). For QP-based grasp optimisation, the friction cone is approximated by a polyhedral cone (4 or 8 linearised faces). The linearisation must be conservative (inner approximation) to guarantee feasibility.

6. Grasp Quality Metrics

Two standard metrics for evaluating grasp quality:

  • ε-metric (Ferrari-Canny): radius of the largest ball centred at the origin that fits inside the convex hull of friction cone face normals in wrench space. Measures the worst-case wrench the grasp can resist. Higher ε = more robust to perturbations.
  • v-metric: volume of the convex hull of friction cone primitives in wrench space. Measures overall grasp versatility — ability to handle a wide range of disturbance wrenches.
Force Closure is Necessary, Not Sufficient A force-closure grasp also needs: (1) kinematic reachability — the hand must be able to reach the contact points given joint limits and the approach direction; (2) no self-collision between fingers; (3) a feasible approach trajectory that does not collide with the object or environment; (4) compatibility with the robot's grasp planner and available hand DOF.
