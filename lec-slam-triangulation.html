Triangulation

Oct 2025 MSc Mobile Robotics, UniBonn

Triangulation recovers the 3D position of a scene point from its observations in two or more images, given the camera matrices. It is a core building block of visual odometry, Structure-from-Motion (SfM), and stereo depth estimation.

1. The Triangulation Problem

Given camera matrices P₁ and P₂ and image observations x₁ and x₂ of the same 3D point X, recover X. In the noiseless case, the rays from C₁ through x₁ and from C₂ through x₂ intersect exactly at X. With image noise, the rays in general do not intersect — we must find X that is "closest" to both rays in some sense.

2. Linear (DLT) Triangulation

The cross-product formulation x × (PX) = 0 yields 2 linear equations per view. From both cameras, stacking gives AX = 0 where A is 4×4. Solve via SVD: X is the last column of V in A = UΣVᵀ. The result is in homogeneous coordinates — dehomogenise by dividing by the last coordinate.

This is simple and fast, but minimises algebraic error (the residual of AX=0) rather than the geometrically meaningful reprojection error.

3. Algebraic vs Geometric Error

Algebraic error (||AX||) is convenient but has no direct geometric interpretation. The reprojection error measures the pixel distance between the observed point and the projection of X back into the image:

e_rep = Σᵢ ||xᵢ − π(Pᵢ, X)||²

Minimising reprojection error gives the maximum-likelihood estimate under Gaussian pixel noise and is the correct criterion for triangulation. However, it requires iterative optimisation.

4. Midpoint Method

An intuitive alternative: find the 3D point that minimises the sum of squared distances to each back-projected ray. Each ray is defined by origin oᵢ = Cᵢ (camera centre) and direction dᵢ = Pᵢ⁺xᵢ (back-projected unit direction). The point on ray i closest to X is oᵢ + tᵢdᵢ where tᵢ = dᵢᵀ(X − oᵢ). The midpoint minimising Σᵢ ||X − (oᵢ + tᵢdᵢ)||² has a closed-form solution using two-ray geometry. For two cameras this is quick and gives a good geometric interpretation.

5. Optimal Triangulation

The optimal solution (Kanatani, Lindstrom) minimises reprojection error exactly. Lindstrom (2010) derived a fast non-iterative solution that corrects the observed image points x₁, x₂ to x̂₁, x̂₂ satisfying the epipolar constraint x̂₂ᵀFx̂₁ = 0 exactly, then applies linear DLT. This is essentially optimal and costs only a few matrix operations — a big improvement over iterative methods.

6. Degenerate Configurations

Triangulation fails or becomes highly inaccurate in several configurations:

  • Forward motion: both cameras moving along the same ray as the observed point. Rays are nearly parallel — depth poorly constrained.
  • Pure rotation: no baseline — no triangulation possible.
  • Far-away points: small parallax → near-parallel rays → high depth uncertainty.
  • Wide baseline: more parallax is better for depth precision, but increases the risk of descriptor mismatch.
Depth Uncertainty vs Distance Depth uncertainty grows quadratically with distance: σ_Z ∝ Z² / (f · B). At twice the range, depth uncertainty is four times worse. In visual odometry systems, landmarks beyond a certain distance are often treated as "infinite" (direction only, no depth) to avoid adding noisy depth estimates into the map.
